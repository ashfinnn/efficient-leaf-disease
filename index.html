CPU-Constrained Deep Learning for Tomato Disease Detection

Benchmarking Traditional (ResNet-50), Modern (ConvNeXt-Tiny), and Hybrid (FastViT-T8) architectures on accessible consumer hardware.

Obidur Rahman
Lipon Chandra Das Arnab Aich Abu Saiman Md Taiham Atif Ibna Latif
Abstract

Tomato cultivation is threatened by yield losses up to 40% due to foliar diseases. While deep learning shows promise, most existing solutions require GPUs unavailable in developing regions. This study evaluates ResNet-50, ConvNeXt-Tiny, and FastViT-T8 under CPU-constrained conditions using the PlantVillage dataset (16,012 images). Results identify FastViT-T8 as the optimal solution (99.66% accuracy, 0.022s inference time), bridging the technological disconnect for resource-poor farmers.

01 Context & Research Gap

Agricultural Impact

Global tomato production exceeds 180 million tons annually. However, foliar diseases like Bacterial Spot and Early Blight cause up to 40% yield loss, threatening the livelihoods of smallholder farmers in South Asia and Africa.

Current detection methods are either too slow (expert visual inspection) or too expensive (laboratory testing), creating a critical need for automated, accessible solutions.

The "GPU Gap" in Prior Work

  • DenseNet (2020): Used Tesla K80 GPU. No CPU benchmarks.
  • TomatoDet (2024): Used Tesla T4. Acknowledged need for lightweight models but didn't test them.
  • Inception V3 (2023): Relied on RTX 3060 Ti.

Our Contribution: First systematic evaluation on consumer CPU (AMD Ryzen 5).

05 Detailed Error Analysis

The "Early Blight" Challenge (200 Validation Samples)

Primary Confusion: Early Blight ‚Üî Late Blight

Both diseases manifest as brown, irregular spots. Without high-resolution texture analysis, models struggle to distinguish the concentric rings of Early Blight from the water-soaked lesions of Late Blight.

Secondary Confusion: Target Spot ‚Üî Septoria

ResNet-50 frequently confused Target Spot with Septoria Leaf Spot due to overlapping visual features (small circular lesions). Modern architectures with better spatial context handled this much better.
